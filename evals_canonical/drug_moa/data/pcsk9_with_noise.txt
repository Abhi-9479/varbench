Gene: PCSK9

Background:
- PCSK9 binds the LDL receptor and promotes its lysosomal degradation;
  higher PCSK9 activity correlates with higher LDL-C.
- Heterozygous loss-of-function (LOF) variant carriers have lower
  LDL-C and lower coronary heart disease risk.

Specific carrier estimates from the literature:
- African-ancestry LOF carriers (Y142X, C679X): -38% LDL-C; CHD HR
  in the original Cohen et al. (NEJM 2006) report ~0.12 over 15 years,
  though sample size was limited and the confidence interval is wide.
- European p.R46L heterozygotes (rs11591147): -15 to -28% LDL-C;
  CHD relative-risk reduction estimates across cohorts range from
  -27% (ARIC) to -47% (Copenhagen General Population Study).
- A small number of published homozygous LOF individuals (n<10 lifetime
  reported worldwide) have LDL-C levels of 14-25 mg/dL with no reported
  adverse phenotype attributable to PCSK9 deficiency.

Counterpoints and caveats to consider:
- Some animal studies have raised theoretical concerns about chronic
  LDL-C reduction and cognitive outcomes, though these have not been
  reproduced in human PCSK9 inhibitor trials.
- A subset of population-genetic analyses using less-curated variant
  sets find effect-size attenuation when adjusting for other lipid loci.
- The clinically approved PCSK9 inhibitors (alirocumab, evolocumab)
  showed CHD benefit in FOURIER and ODYSSEY OUTCOMES, but the absolute
  risk reduction was modest (~1.5-2 percentage points over 2-3 years)
  and questions remain about cost-effectiveness at population scale.
- A 2020 review noted that "not every LOF observation in human genetics
  translates to a successful therapeutic strategy" - citing, among
  other examples, the CETP cautionary tale.

A pharmaceutical company is reviewing PCSK9 as a target. Choose exactly
one of:
  - 'agonist'   : a drug that increases PCSK9 activity
  - 'inhibitor' : a drug that decreases PCSK9 activity
  - 'unclear'   : the human-genetics evidence does not cleanly favor either direction
